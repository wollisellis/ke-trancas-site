## 1. Introduction

The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) represents a paradigm shift in artificial intelligence applications for healthcare. Models such as GPT-4, BERT, and their clinical variants have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding and generating human-like text, opening new possibilities for clinical practice, research, and education.

### 1.1 Background

Healthcare generates enormous volumes of unstructured data—clinical notes, research papers, patient communications—that traditional systems struggle to process efficiently. LLMs offer a solution through natural language understanding and generation capabilities.

### 1.2 Objectives

This systematic review aims to:
1. Catalog current applications of LLMs in healthcare
2. Evaluate evidence on effectiveness and safety
3. Identify challenges and future directions

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Principal Findings

This review demonstrates that LLMs are being applied across diverse healthcare domains, from clinical documentation to drug discovery. The technology shows particular promise in:

1. **Automating clinical documentation** - reducing administrative burden
2. **Supporting diagnostic decisions** - providing second opinions
3. **Enhancing medical education** - interactive learning tools
4. **Accelerating drug discovery** - literature synthesis and hypothesis generation

### 4.2 Limitations

**Study limitations:**
- Rapidly evolving field makes comprehensive coverage difficult
- Many studies are descriptive rather than evaluative
- Lack of standardized outcome measures

**LLM limitations:**
- Hallucination risk in clinical contexts
- Limited domain-specific training
- Privacy and security concerns

### 4.3 Future Directions

Key areas for future research:
- Development of clinical-specific LLMs
- Robust evaluation frameworks
- Integration with clinical workflows
- Ethical and regulatory guidelines

## 5. Conclusions

Large Language Models represent a significant opportunity for healthcare transformation. However, responsible deployment requires addressing accuracy, bias, and regulatory challenges. Future work should focus on rigorous evaluation and transparent reporting of LLM performance in clinical settings.
